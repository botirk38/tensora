--- MAIN.TEX ABSTRACT ---

Inference on large language models is often gated by loading weights from storage 
into GPU memory. In serverless and multi-tenant settings, idle models are evicted 
and must be reloaded on demand, so cold-start latency can hinge on how checkpoints 
are read. Peak NVMe bandwidth is only an upper bound: request structure, checkpoint 
layout, and software above the block layer often dominate realised throughput.

This paper treats checkpoint loading as a measurement-driven systems problem. Using 
TensorStore as a controlled experimental vehicle, we compare synchronous POSIX I/O, 
Tokio-based async loading, io_uring, and an adaptive default policy across contiguous 
SafeTensors and partitioned ServerlessLLM-style layouts, then check how those choices 
survive Python bindings and vLLM integration.

Findings. For contiguous SafeTensors, explicit synchronous loading wins at the small 
end of our ladder, while explicit io_uring wins at the largest multi-shard scales 
where queue depth matters. For partitioned ServerlessLLM, synchronous loading is 
never fastest in our matrix; the relevant trade-off is among async, io_uring, and 
default. These layout- and scale-dependent crossovers show that no single fixed 
backend is the right default.

The paper's central contribution is therefore an operational policy claim: backend 
choice for LLM checkpoint loading should be format-aware and adaptive, with explicit 
overrides, rather than treated as one universal loader decision. Python and vLLM 
results support the same point by showing that integration overhead can reorder 
backend rankings and that loader choice matters most for initialisation and 
time-to-first-token, not steady-state decode. Appendix~documented harnesses, 
result paths, and environment fields to record for replication.

--- ARXIV_PLAIN.TXT ABSTRACT ---

Inference on large language models is often gated by loading weights from storage 
into GPU memory. In serverless and multi-tenant settings, idle models are evicted 
and must be reloaded on demand, so cold-start latency can hinge on how checkpoints 
are read. Peak NVMe bandwidth is only an upper bound: request structure, checkpoint 
layout, and software above the block layer often dominate realised throughput.

We present TensorStore, an open checkpoint-loading framework and evaluation harness 
for Linux that supports SafeTensors and a partitioned ServerlessLLM-style layout, 
with eager backends spanning synchronous POSIX I/O, Tokio-based async loading, and 
io_uring. Python bindings and a custom vLLM integration extend the study beyond 
Rust-only microbenchmarks. Experiments use six public checkpoints and both layouts.

Findings. For contiguous SafeTensors, explicit synchronous loading wins at the small 
end of our ladder, while explicit io_uring wins at the largest multi-shard scales 
where queue depth matters. No single fixed backend tracks that crossover, so an 
adaptive default policy (selector plus planning) is justified; it is not equivalent 
to any one explicit column.

For partitioned ServerlessLLM, synchronous loading is never fastest in our matrix; 
the relevant trade-off is among async, io_uring, and default. Python adds enough 
overhead to reorder some Rust-level conclusions unless the bindings are tuned. 
In vLLM, loader choice affects initialisation and time-to-first-token far more than 
steady-state decode. The operational recommendation is a format-aware adaptive 
default with explicit overrides, not one universal backend. The reproducibility 
appendix documents harnesses, result paths, and environment fields to record for 
replication.
