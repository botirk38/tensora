\subsection{Headline findings}

Two patterns emerge from the Rust matrices. First, SafeTensors loading consistently favours async over sync, with the advantage growing from 1.13$\times$ at 0.6B to 2.40$\times$ at 32B. Second, ServerlessLLM-style partitioning similarly favours async at every scale; sync never wins when the read geometry is range-dominated. The format, not the backend name, explains the reversal.

\subsection{Rust Backend Results}
\label{sec:rust-results}

fraction of total startup.

\subsection{Layer summary}

Four principles emerge. (1) Backend ranking depends on checkpoint format: async outperforms sync on both SafeTensors and ServerlessLLM at the raw I/O level, with the advantage growing with model scale. (2) The magnitude of advantage differs by format: SafeTensors shows larger speedups (up to 2.40$\times$) because its contiguous read pattern benefits more from async's concurrent scheduling at scale. (3) Tensora's async backend is 1.6--13.8$\times$ faster than the hf-native baseline, confirming that the framework provides practical improvement over stock Python-level loading. (4) At the vLLM serving level, raw loading gains are partially absorbed by engine overhead (graph construction, KV-cache, autotuning); the advantage is most visible for smaller models where loading dominates initialization.
