\subsection{Headline findings}

SafeTensors loading favours async over sync at every scale, with the advantage growing from 1.13$\times$ at 0.6B to 2.40$\times$ at 32B. ServerlessLLM-style partitioning similarly favours async at every scale; sync never wins when the read geometry is range-dominated. The format, not the backend name, explains the reversal.

\label{sec:rust-results}

At 0.6\,B, \texttt{ts\_safetensors\_async} achieves 11.8\,s vs.\ 39.6\,s
for native (3.4$\times$ speedup). At $\geq$4\,B, native is competitive
or faster: engine overhead (model-graph construction, KV-cache allocation,
FlashInfer autotuning) dominates the initialization budget. At 32\,B,
native (44.2\,s) outperforms all Tensora paths (best: 75.6\,s).

Native shows low run-to-run CV (3.7\% at 32B). Tensora loaders show
higher variance at some scales (ts\_safetensors\_sync CV 92\% at 32B
due to a 607\,s outlier). Rankings are stable after excluding
first-run outliers.

\subsection{Layer summary}

(1) Async outperforms sync on both formats at the raw I/O level, with the advantage growing with model scale. (2) SafeTensors shows larger speedups (up to 2.40$\times$) than ServerlessLLM (up to 1.46$\times$). (3) Tensora async is 1.6--13.8$\times$ faster than hf-native. (4) At the vLLM level, engine overhead absorbs raw loading gains at $\geq$4\,B; the advantage is largest for smaller models.
