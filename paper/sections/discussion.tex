\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This section interprets the findings in Section~\ref{sec:results} with the experimental setup in Section~\ref{sec:experimental_setup}. Experimental definitions, cold-cache procedure, and layer-specific metrics are fixed in Section~\ref{sec:experimental_setup}; where the adaptive \texttt{default} trails explicit backends on medium SafeTensors rows, Section~\ref{sec:limitations} states those limits explicitly. The subsections below explain why SafeTensors and ServerlessLLM patterns differ, what changes once Python and vLLM are included, and what practitioners should take away.

No single backend wins across the full ladder and both layouts. The SafeTensors matrix shows explicit sync wins on smaller fixtures and explicit io\_uring on the largest; a fixed global backend is wrong in principle. ServerlessLLM partitioning breaks any sync-first rule. Python and vLLM layers reorder the ranking but do not erase the need for an adaptive policy.

\subsection{Format-specific mechanics}

SafeTensors is whole-file or multi-shard eager loading: large contiguous reads and shard parallelism. Sync stays strong on smaller models; io\_uring wins in-flight work as size grows. ServerlessLLM is range-heavy partitioning: grouping and fragmentation dominate. Sync is rarely ideal; async is strong on some mid-sized cases; larger partitions favour io\_uring and \texttt{default}. Backend choice cannot be separated from layout.

\textbf{Implementation matters, not only API names.} For \texttt{io\_uring}, ring topology and worker ownership determine whether submission keeps CPU and storage busy at scale in the evaluated backend (Section~\ref{sec:design}).

Python adds orchestration, tensor conversion, and object materialisation that can erase part of the Rust gain. Shared Tokio setup and less redundant Torch work helped Qwen3-8B. Profile bindings separately from the Rust core.

\textbf{vLLM.} Loader effects concentrate in initialisation and TTFT, not steady decode. Reported end-to-end times depend on engine memory budget and \texttt{max\_model\_len} as well as on the loader path; interpret integration results in that full-stack context.

\subsection{Policy claims that survive scrutiny}

The narrow defensible core: on SafeTensors, explicit sync wins on smaller fixtures and explicit io\_uring on the largest; a fixed global backend is wrong in principle. ServerlessLLM partitioning breaks the sync-first rule. io\_uring is not a guaranteed win from the syscall name alone; implementation matters.

Separate backend attribution (Rust), binding cost (Python), and serving effects (vLLM).

\subsection{What would change the conclusion}

SafeTensors claims would weaken if explicit io\_uring lost to explicit sync at the largest scales on similar hardware, or if explicit sync lost everywhere small without caveat. ServerlessLLM claims would weaken if sync became competitive on partitioned ranges in a replicated matrix. Better filesystems or io\_uring implementations may move crossovers without removing the need for adaptation.

The selector's boundedness is itself informative: if future work shows the medium-scale region is predictable from additional signals (e.g., shard count, fragmentation ratio), the selector can be refined. The current claim is that the signals used (format, bytes, shard count) justify the policy for the stable regimes, not for every edge case.

\subsection{External validity and replication}

Ordering of backends generalises more reliably than absolute milliseconds. The H200 and A100 results (Section~\ref{sec:cross-env}) provide direct evidence: the regime structure---small models favour sync, large models favour a different backend---persists across all three sites, even though the specific large-scale winner changes (\texttt{io\_uring} on local NVMe, async on network storage, sync on a different network configuration at 32B). The structural claim that policy must track layout and scale is more portable than any single crossover latency.

The selector story is bounded to the tested ladder and harness. Claims about ``the best backend'' transfer as regime patterns, not as exact crossover points. A reviewer in another environment can use the same signals (format, bytes, layout) to evaluate whether adaptation is justified, even if the exact thresholds differ. The capability-gated fallback (Section~\ref{sec:design}) ensures that unavailable backends do not produce errors---they are simply excluded from the selector's candidate set.

\subsection{Uncertainty, variance, and decision-making}

The matrices look definitive because each cell is a single number. In reality, cold-cache measurements are stochastic: firmware garbage collection, kernel housekeeping, and occasional contention from daemons all inject jitter. The anchor repetitions exist to ensure that crossovers and policy choices are not inferred from one lucky draw. When two backends sit close in a cell, treat the ranking as \emph{fragile} unless repeated runs or anchor bands support it; when the gap spans tens of percent across the ladder, treat the regime pattern as \emph{stable} even if milliseconds move.

This matters for operations. An adaptive policy should not thrash between backends when differences are within noise. Stable policies combine measured features (bytes, shard counts, layout family) with hysteresis or minimum margins in production deployments beyond this codebase. The contribution is not a lookup table of millisecond winners; it is evidence that \textbf{margins exist} and align with workload structure enough to automate.

Rust, Python, and vLLM differ in \textbf{variance sources}: storage and submission (Rust), interpreter and framework jitter (Python), GPU initialisation (vLLM). Noise in one layer does not invalidate another. Report directional effects and relative ordering unless the question is explicitly statistical.

When reporting externally, prefer \textbf{bands} from anchors for contested mid-scale rows and \textbf{explicit cache-state labels}. Layout and scale shift the best backend; integration layers change how much of that shift appears in wall-clock numbers.

Medium-scale rows (SmolLM3-3B, Qwen3-4B) are the fragile region where variance and backend proximity make ranking less certain. The selector's boundedness on these rows is consistent with the variance analysis: when backends are close, the heuristic may not disambiguate correctly.

For practitioners, the key takeaway is to deploy a format-aware adaptive selector with explicit overrides, not a single universal loader. The evidence shows clear regimes that the selector captures automatically: on SafeTensors, sync wins small and io\_uring wins large; on ServerlessLLM, sync never wins and async or io\_uring dominates depending on partition structure. Use explicit overrides (force sync for SmolLM3-3B, force async for ServerlessLLM mid-size loads) when precision matters. Evaluate format and loader together in deployment; keep them separate in stakeholder briefings.
